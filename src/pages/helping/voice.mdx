# Plain License Voice and Messaging Guidelines

We want our message to be clear and accessible. To do that, we use a voice that is friendly, rebellious, and empowering in everything we write.

## Our Voice

We challenge the idea that legal documents have to be confusing. We do it with warmth, not hostility. Think of it this way: we're the friend who explains the fine print in plain terms, not the angry protester shouting outside a courthouse.

Here's what that looks like:

- **Rebellious yet Professional**: We challenge legal complexity head-on, but we stay credible. We don't bash lawyers or the legal system — we show there's a better way.
- **Empowering**: We give people control through understanding. We don't "dumb down" — we make things clearer so people can make their own informed decisions.
- **Inclusive**: We write for everyone. We avoid jargon, idioms, and cultural references that not everyone would understand.
- **Friendly**: We're conversational, like talking to a friend. We use "you" and "we" because we're on the same team.
- **Transparent**: We're open about our process, our decisions, and our limitations. We don't hide behind vague language.

## Our Tone

Our tone shifts slightly depending on context, but it always stays approachable:

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| License text | Clear, precise, confident |
| Site content | Warm, conversational, encouraging |
| Legal discussions | Respectful, thoughtful, open-minded |
| Contributor guides | Supportive, patient, welcoming |

## Writing Tips

- **Use active voice**: "You can share the work" not "The work may be shared by the licensee."
- **Keep sentences short**: One idea per sentence. If a sentence has a comma and a conjunction, consider splitting it.
- **Use "you" and "we"**: "You" for the reader. "We" for Plain License. "We, the authors" for licensors.
- **Avoid jargon**: If you must use a technical term, explain it right away in simple language.
- **Be direct**: Say what you mean. Don't hedge with "it should be noted that" or "it is worth mentioning."

## Before and After

Here's what our voice sounds like in practice:

| Before (legal voice) | After (Plain License voice) |
|---|---|
| "The licensee is hereby granted a non-exclusive, worldwide, royalty-free license..." | "You are free to use this work anywhere, for any purpose, at no cost." |
| "Subject to the conditions set forth herein..." | "As long as you follow these rules..." |
| "The above copyright notice and this permission notice shall be included in all copies or substantial portions of the Software." | "When you share the work or a big part of it, include this license and the copyright notice." |
| "THE SOFTWARE IS PROVIDED 'AS IS', WITHOUT WARRANTY OF ANY KIND..." | "We offer the work 'as is' with no warranties. We are not responsible for any damages or issues." |

## Our Preferred Words

We use consistent words across all our content:

| We say | Instead of | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| you | licensee, recipient | |
| we, the authors | licensor, provider, owner | Use full form on first mention per license, then just "we" |
| the work | licensed work, software, creation, covered work | On site pages, "your work" or "your project" is also fine |
| source materials | source code, source form | Not that "source code" is complex — it just limits scope to software |
| share | distribute, redistribute, convey | |
| change | modify, alter, amend | |
| give | grant, convey | |
| use | utilize, exploit, exercise | |
| promises | warranties, guarantees | |
| rules | conditions, limitations | For things you **must do** |
| restrictions | *(keep as-is)* | For things you **cannot do** — distinct from "rules" |
| recraft | rewrite, adapt, translate | Our signature verb for what we do to licenses |

## Tips for Headers

Use empowering framing when you write headers. Instead of "Limitations on Use," try "What You Can Do." Instead of "Conditions," try "Your Responsibilities." Make people feel like the license is *for* them, not *against* them.

By following these guidelines, you help us create a voice that people trust and understand.
